Pinky Swear Android App

Let's Get Productive!

Overview & Goal

Pinky Swear is an Android mobile application that strives to offer users a novel and fun way to stay productive. Initially developed as a side project, the primary idea was to allow a user (i.e. User A) make a "promise" to another randomly matched user (i.e. User B), who in turn will also have to make a promise to User A.

A specific timeframe of 24 hours will be set for both users, and throughout the course, User A and B will have the chance to communicate with each another via a chat messenging tool - giving them a chance to encourage one another to fulfill his/her promise.

The catch? Once User A fulfills his/her promise in action, only User B can check it off on the app - thereby giving the app a collaborative nature overall. In a way, you can refer to Pinky Swear as a "collaborative to-do-list" app.

User Research

Quantitative user research was conducted in the form of a Survey. A total of 50 people were randomly chosen from friend and family circles, as well as the public. Below is a summary analysis of the process, along with important questions asked, associated responses, and data findings.

From the survey, 3 important numbers that support the concept behind Pinky Swear receive the most attention:

From the indications above, the last finding proves to be particularly important as it revolves around a core feature of Pinky Swear - to make promises with one another and engage in a collaborative process in order to encourage each other's fulfillment of the respective promises - thereby illustrating a big opportunity.

Through the research, it is also interesting to find that our largest target audience consists of females aged between 18 and 49, and those who typically possess 1-4 actions they have to complete as well as be reminded of per day.

Ideation/Sketches

Early Sketches were followed after some brainstorming sessions.

User Flow/Wireframes

Based on the user research, feedback, and early sketches - a high-fidelity User Flow and a set of Wireframes were created for Usability Testing purposes.

The following are important design decisions made:

  • Provide "Sign Up With Facebook" as the only available registration option to speed up the onboarding process - making it the more easy and convenient for users to start using the app.
  • Provide 2 methods for creating promises:
    • Using "predefined promises" suggested by the app in order to get users started with Pinky Swear, while giving them a good idea of the promise types they could make.
    • Customizing their own promises and essentially creating whatever the users wish.
  • Allow users to see the exact number of promises they've fulfilled ever since the app's initial download. This will serve as the motivational force.
  • Allow users to communicate with their matched partners via a chat messaging tool, in order to enhance the "fun" aspect. Once a promise is fulfilled in action, only the user's partner can check it off by tapping the "Fulfill" button.
  • Allow users to check the current progress of their promises, their matched partners' promises, and time left to fulfill the promises. At the same time, allow users to review past promises that either failed or were successfully fulfilled. These will serve as the "Overview" dashboards for users to get a good grasp of where they stand.

Prototype/Usability Testing

Using the wireframes above, an Interactive Prototype was created to test on 10 users. Their feedback was then incorporated into the next design iteration.

The following are key changes:

  • On the screen where users can see the exact number of promises they've fulfilled ever since the app's download, a small motivational message can accompany the number. Depending on the range within which the number is located, the message could be different. In this regards, the respective ranges would be:
    • 1 to 24
    • 25 to 49
    • 50 to 74
    • 75 to 100
    • 100+
  • Allow users to add images and emoticons within the chat messaging tool. Images can help with the "evidence" factor, giving users a way to prove that they have indeed carried out an action and fulfilled their promises. Emoticons will help to make the chats more fun and inviting.
  • Include "Popular" as one of the promise categories. This will contain the most commonly used promises within the Pinky Swear community.
  • White background with soft flat colors on top to complement the clean and minimalist design concept.
  • Big, bold buttons and generally easily identifiable action items.

High-Fidelity Designs - Final

Releases

After roughly 4 months of design, development, as well as marketing efforts, the 1st version of Pinky Swear was released onto Google Play in June 2015. While the initial plan was to only build the app for fun, opportunities where Pinky Swear's current as well as potential features could be further developed were explored.

A 2nd round of usability testing was conducted using the functional app (5 participants), and below are key changes/additions:

  • Implement a "match up with Facebook friends" feature. This will allow users who feel uncomfortable about being matched with strangers to find partners within their friend or family circles.
  • Implement a pop-up notification which says "Well done! You've just fulfilled a promise" after the user's partner checks off his/her promise. This can appear on any screens in the app, or even outside of the app.
  • Implement pop-up notifications that inform users of promise deadlines approaching, AND promises that have passed deadlines.
  • Implement a simple 1-page tutorial that will teach users how to use Pinky Swear. This screen will be automatically visible to newly registered users as soon as they log in for the first time.
    • Implement a "Tutorial" button located next to the "Profile" button. This will allow users to access the Tutorial page at all time.

Feedback from the usability test allowed us to release Pinky Swear V.2 in November 2015.

Update

As of March 2016, the decision had been made to discontinue Pinky Swear's operation, due to a lack of market interest. With well over 500 downloads throughout its lifetime, this concluded our one year of experience with this very special project - one that had taught us a great lesson about the end-to-end cycle of agile product development. More importantly, we've given ourselves the chance to appreciate the significance of teamwork and collaboration as a component of success.

With Pinky Swear, I've personally had the opportunity to ship my first ever design as a UX professional. As such, the app will always maintain a very special spot in my heart. Thank you to everyone who has shown and continues to offer your support to Pinky Swear. This experience wouldn't have been possible without all of you. :)

Husky Yum iOS App

Bringing Students to Healthy Meals

Problem Statement

Eating healthy is a challenge for a lot of students. For many, it is difficult to make time for nutritious meals in between classes and work. For others, a limited budget might be keeping them from actively choosing healthy food. This food culture enables a student body that prioritizes speed and convenience over health and food quality.

This project strives to gain a better understanding of the many barriers students face in making healthy food choices as they make their way around campus. Through various research methods, we want to learn the variety of behaviors that students exhibit towards healthy food options, and by doing so, our goal is to design an engaging solution that will help students access healthier, more informed, and wholesome food within the UW vicinity.

User Research

Three primary research methods were utilized throughout the research process, including Interviews, Surveys, and "Fly-On-The-Wall" Observations.

Using sticky notes, each person posted on the board comments, patterns, and information we gathered from the researches. We then sorted them into groups based on intuitive relationships. This allowed us to come up with five groups in accordance with these themes:

  1. Time
  2. Social
  3. Attitudes toward Food
  4. Uncertainties
  5. Lifestyle

Usability Testing

Using the "Think-Aloud Protocol" Method, a usability test was conducted with a total number of 7 UW students. The test focused on the user’s ability to search, post and utilize other product features. Usability test tasks were designed to assess the accessibility and understandability of the end-to-end user flow for the app's major features.

Participants of the usability test were both UW undergraduate and graduate students interested in healthy eating on and around campus.

Concept-wise, the test revealed users' desire to see a focus on not only homemade meals/recipes, but also meals that they may discover from eating out at restaurants, bistros, cafes etc. In this regards, the ability to find, discover, and share healthy "menu items" was an important addition of which the usability test has provided.

On the other hand, below is a list of other key findings and changes made:

  1. ISSUE: Users are confused about the process of sharing a new homemade recipe.

    2. SOLUTIONS

    • Replace the word "Share" with "Post"
    • Implement a vivid "Post" button on the "Trending" page.
    • Remove the ability to share and ask about existing recipes.
    • Allow users to access the "Search" and "Post" features from the burger menu.
    • Develop the burger menu to include all of these options: My Profile, Search Recipes, Search Menu Items, Post Recipes, and Post Menu Items.
  2. ISSUE: Users can't locate the "Search" feature to filter and find recipes.

    3. SOLUTIONS

    • Remove fixed menu located at the bottom of the screen.
    • Implement a search bar at the top of every page (except for "Trending" page.
    • On the "Trending" page, put the "Post" and "Search" buttons next to each other.
  3. ISSUE: Users don't quite understand what the "Trending" page means (where the top meals as rated by the Husky Yum community are listed).

    4. SOLUTIONS

    • Redesign the "Trending" page's user interface to make it more visual with less text.
    • Include a photo of the homademade recipe or menu item in question.
    • Present only the top 5 homademade recipes or menu items as rated by the HuskyYum community.
    • Allow users to easily navigage through the top 5 homemade recipes or menu items by swiping right or left.
    • Implement two tabs close to the top of the screen, "Homemade" and "Eat-out", so users can conveniently view either the top 5 homemade recipes or menu items.
  4. ISSUE: The "Add to Shopping List" feature - where users can add a specific recipe's ingredients to a personal shopping list for review - is only visible after some deep scrolling.

    5. SOLUTIONS

    • Remove the ability to collectively add all the ingredients included in a homemade recipe or menu item to the shopping list.
    • Allow users to add individual ingredients to the shopping list.
  5. ISSUE: Users can't go back to the previous page when they want to.

    6. SOLUTIONS

    • Add a "Back" button on the top-left corner of every page.
    • Transition the burger menu icon/button to the top-right corner.
  6. ISSUE: The context of the "Dietary Filter" isn't clear on what some of the filters will screen out.

    7. SOLUTION

    • Change the wording of "Dairy", "Egg", "Gluten", and "Peanut" to "Dairy-free", "Egg-free", "Gluten-free", and "Peanut-free".
  7. ISSUE: Users find the "Calendar" feature overall confusing and unnecessary.

    8. SOLUTION

    • Remove the "Calendar" feature.

Reimagining INRIX Traffic

A Smarter Way To Drive

Project Overview

In May 2016, I had the chance to attend a UX Design workshop which focuses on the topic of "Aligning Organizational Vision With Product Design". Essentially, the objective of the workshop is to train designers in the art of designing better products while simultaneously & fully optimizing the organizational vision as a solid foundation to base of their design decisions.

As part of the final project, we were asked to conduct two exercises. First, a Comparative Analysis between two products that operate within the same space and yet possess different design rationale due to the differing visions behind their production. Second, a "Design Reimagination" in which we were to pick one product between the two and take it through a quick design process in order to uncover a small sample of usability issues, and accordingly to produce a set of redesign ideas to solve these issues.

Part 1 - Comparative Analysis

For my Comparative Analysis, I've picked two mobile applications that operate within the Traffic Navigation industry, Google Maps & INRIX Traffic. The choice was mostly a personal preference. I've always been a big fan of cars & automobiles, and recently I've discovered an interest for Big Data & Machine Learning. Combining these components together - along with my passion for UX Design - and the decision to challenge myself with these two apps is almost a no-brainer.

Note that for the purposes of this project, only the ANDROID version of both apps are used.

Google Maps VS. INRIX Traffic

Most of us are fairly familiar with Google Maps. It is a global web mapping service developed by Google which offers satellite imagery, street maps, 360 degrees paranomic view, real-time traffic conditions, and route planning for travelling by foot, car, bicycle, or public transportation. Google Maps, in essence, is a one-stop-shop for all things physical navigation & positioning, and is available on both web & mobile platforms - the latter of which is chosen for the purpose of this project's analysis.

INRIX Traffic is a mobile application developed by INRIX, a global company which provides a variety of services pertaining to road traffic & driver services. In a nutshell, INRIX provides historical, real-time traffic information, traffic forecasts, travel times, travel time polygons and traffic count to businesses & individuals. It also gathers, curates and reports roadway incidents such as traffic collisions, road closures and road works. Specifically within the context of INRIX Traffic, the app optimizes traffic & GPS data from a global network of over 250 million connected vehicles, roadways and cities to improve users' commute by providing the best routes & directions to new & familiar destinations.

Below is a small Comparative Analysis table that illustrates from a high-level perspective the feature offerings shared (or not) by Google Maps & INRIX Traffic.

From the first glance, INRIX Traffic may present itself as being inferior to the giant that is Google Maps. However, a deep dig into the differing vision that lies behind these two products will help to explain the scope & breadth of each app as a player within the Traffic Navigation industry, and accordingly their unique value propositions.

In this respect, while Google Map strives to offer users an integrated toolset of general mapping services that can provide them access to an enormous set of data regarding the geographic layout of the world - from traffic information to your local sushi restaurant - INRIX strictly focuses on the realm of traffic intelligence & management.

As one large corporation, Google's mission is to organize all the world's information into a single platform. As a member of this big family, Google Maps' goal is to take the world's physical information and make it accessible and useful to everyone. On the other hand, INRIX's mission for INRIX Traffic is a lot simpler and perfectly focused. In the words Bryan Mystele, President & CEO of INRIX:

"We designed INRIX Traffic with one specific vision: To help drivers move through their daily lives as quickly and efficiently as possible".

Design Rationale

With this understanding of the two apps' individual market position, the next step in the process is to analyze the differences that exist between their designs. Remember that while the aim of Google Maps is to provide users an end-to-end physical information-accessing platform, INRIX Traffic's primary objective is to offer drivers (and specifically drivers only) a tool that can help them make better driving decisions with the help of real-time traffic information.

As such, since INRIX Traffic is very specifically catered to drivers - who most likely would find themselves in need of the app while on the road, given the constantly changing traffic conditions in metropolitan cities - the app should be optimally designed so that it is quick & easy to use. Furthermore, this quick & easy characteristic should also be prioritized under the objective of helping drivers focus on the road rather than their phone screens. Number of steps to complete a task should be kept to the minimum, and design components should be as intuitive & efficient as possible.

In this respect, I've picked out two specific design components which - in my belief - speak up largely to this "quick & easy" principle that defines the INRIX Traffic experience.

  1. The Zoom-In & Zoom-Out Buttons
  2. The Voice-Assistant Activation/Deactivation Toggle

Zoom-In & Zoom-Out

Users have various reasons why they may want to zoom-in or zoom-out of the map. Frequently, they might have to do this while driving. With Google Maps, users will have to directly interact with the screen via their hand gestures to accomplish this task. With INRIX Traffic, the two "Add" & "Minus" buttons have made this a much simpler action.

Activate/Deactivate Voice Assistant

The Voice Assistant is a central component to any GPS tool. Both Google Maps & INRIX Traffic effectively employ this feature. However, for different reasons, having a Voice Assistant isn't something that is always desired, and of course, turning the Voice Assistant off (or on again) is plausible with both apps. With INRIX Traffic, it is an easy toggle switch. With Google Maps, on the other hand, the presence of an extra feature has created an additional step in this process for users.

Part 2 - Reimagining INRIX Traffic

For the second part of this project, the goal is to take the current INRIX Traffic App through a short design process in order to uncover certain usability issues, as well as come up with potential solution suggestions. In this respect, the process is as follows:

Summary of Usability Issues & Proposed Solutions

Through a combination of Heuristic Analysis & Usability Interviews (Test), these are revealed to be the most important issues:

ISSUE 1: Ineffective Feedback After Adding A New Trip

Screen: TRIPS

Suggestion: After the new trip is added, the calendar should jump to the corresponding date on which this trip was set up for.

ISSUE 2: Navigation Settings Not Available For Individual Route

Screen: MAP

Suggestion: Allow users to apply Navigation Settings for each specific route. Implement a "Filter" button that is always accessible on the map so users can change their Navigation Settings wherever & whenever.

ISSUE 3: Heavy Information & Unclean Layout On "Places" Screen

Screen: PLACES

Suggestion: Consider hiding traffic information under its corresponding place using the "Pop-Up, Pop-Down" functionality (i.e. if a user wants to see traffic information for Bellevue College, he/she can tap on Kirkland Library and open a pop-up box which contains the relevant data, and accordingly tap anywhere to close the pop-up).

Other Suggestions

Lastly, a number of minor suggestions that can be optimized to improve the overall Traffic App experience.

  • Consider exploring other icon designs for "Compass" & "Route" on the "Map" screen which are more intuitive to users - thereby improving usage recognition.
  • When adding a new place on the "Places" screen, consider exploring another icon design for "Other" that are more intuitive to users than "Star". Again, usage recognition is potentially enhanced.
  • Consider redesigning the "Line Bars" which represent the traffic condition of potential routes to take. Many participants from the interview have expressed that the current design may seem a bit old-fashioned & unclean. Material Design could be explored for its minimalist & clean concept.

From a personal perspective, this project has offered me a truly educational experience. Final Designs

Due to the project's strict NDA, I won't be able to showcase many of the design assets and/or data created throughout the project's timeline. Nonetheless, below is one particular example of an important design decision made regarding the Content Positioning of VnExpress and its international counterpart. While most of the important design differences revolve around the Information Architecture & Content Strategy between the two versions, this feature has emerged as the most interesting component to me.

VIETNAMESE

From our user research (primarily through Eye-Tracking/Heat Map Analysis methods), it was revealed that a lot of Vietnamese readers have the tendency to view VnExpress's content via the F-Shaped Pattern - a concept that was first explored by UX expert Jakob Nielsen, who has conducted numerous tests on the ways users read web content. Essentially:

  • Users first read in a horizontal movement, usually across the upper part of the content area. This initial element forms the F's top bar.
  • Next, users move down the page a bit and then read across in a second horizontal movement that typically covers a shorter area than the previous movement. This additional element forms the F's lower bar.
  • Finally, users scan the content's left side in a vertical movement. Sometimes this is a fairly slow and systematic scan that appears as a solid stripe on an eyetracking heatmap. Other times users move faster, creating a spottier heatmap. This last element forms the F's stem.

(From "F-Shaped Pattern For Reading Web Content" by Jakob Nielsen)

As such, our research data has shown that Vietnamese users like to see content blocks listed in a vertical manner, in which they can quickly scroll through the site and browse for their preferred content. This is evident in both the Home Page & Article Page of the Vietnamese site.

INTERNATIONAL

On the other hand, international readers have illustrated a mixed approach to viewing web content. While some follow the aforementioned F-Shaped Pattern, a significantly large portion of others have delineated a pattern that's more "Z-Shaped" in nature. In essence, users' content-viewing behavior is still naturally fast, in which they would typically begin by skimming quickly horizontally across the page. Nonetheless, rather than immediately moving on to scanning the content in a vertical movement, there's a tendency to prolong the original horizontal approach in an attempt to seek out for more interesting articles.

Accordingly, a design decision has been made to implement a hybrid pattern where components of both the F-Shape & Z-Shape frameworks are optimized for VnExpress International. As one can see, content blocks are positioned in such a way that will allow for both effective vertical & horizontal viewing.
